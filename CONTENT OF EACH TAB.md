INTRODUCTION: CHAPTER 1

A. Importance of Internship
The importance of an internship (or On-the-Job Training) is that it gives students real-world experience in their chosen field. It allows them to apply the knowledge and skills they learned in school into actual work situations, making learning more practical and meaningful.

Internship also helps students develop important skills such as communication, teamwork, discipline, time management, and professionalism. It exposes them to the real working environment, helping them understand workplace expectations and responsibilities.

Most importantly, internship prepares students for their future careers by building their confidence and experience, making it easier for them to adjust when they enter the workforce after graduation.

B. Objectives of Internship

The objectives of this internship are the following:

A. To provide students with real-world experience related to their chosen field and help them apply the knowledge they learned in school into actual work situations.

B. To develop and enhance students’ technical, practical, and professional skills through hands-on training and exposure to workplace tasks.

C. To improve important work values such as discipline, responsibility, communication skills, teamwork, and time management.

D. To help students become familiar with the workplace environment, understand organizational procedures, and learn how to handle different tasks and challenges.

E. To prepare students for their future careers by building their confidence, experience, and readiness for employment after graduation.

D. History/ Background of the Agency
HISTORY
OMECO, or Occidental Mindoro Electric Cooperative, Inc., was organized in 1974 through the initiative of Atty. Eufrocino L. Ramos under Presidential Decree No. 269, which created the National Electrification Administration in 1969 to oversee rural electrification nationwide. With an initial ₱6.9 million loan from NEA in March 1974, OMECO’s interim board headed by Dr. Rodrigo M. David purchased the San Jose Electric Company for ₱620,000 and formally took over operations on May 7, 1974. Under its first General Manager, Engr. Alberto A. Guiang, the coop expanded from just nine employees, acquired two 350kw generators, built transmission and distribution lines, and launched aggressive membership drives. Public awareness efforts included the first Electrification Week in 1975 and annual membership meetings that encouraged community involvement through the bayanihan system.

Through the late 1970s and 1980s, OMECO continued expanding its coverage, energizing Mamburao in 1980 and acquiring Silahis Electric Company that same year. The coop built its main office in Pulang Lupa, Central in 1978 and experimented with projects like the Dendro Thermal Plant in Paclolo, Magsaysay  the first of its kind in Asia  though the Ipil-Ipil plantation for fuel was later destroyed. By December 1983, OMECO faced a severe financial crisis with no aid from NEA and unpaid wages. Col. Zoilo M. Perez (Ret.) took over as General Manager, paying employees from his own funds and forgoing his salary for six months. Through strict frugality and employee cooperation, operations resumed in March 1984 and the coop eventually recovered, earning Perez the nickname “Wonder Lolo” and multiple awards for collection efficiency and management.

In 1995, Alex C. Labrador became the first rank-and-file employee to rise to General Manager, continuing energization efforts in remote areas like Botasan, Monte Claro, and Sitio Cebu in 1996. A major policy debate arose in 1997 over whether to register with the Cooperative Development Authority or remain under NEA. In a membership vote on February 21, 1997, 444 voted to stay with NEA versus 104 for CDA, preserving OMECO’s tax exemptions and commitment to serve non-profitable far-flung barangays. OMECO celebrated its 25th anniversary in 1999 and by 2000 remained focused on its mission of providing dependable service to member-consumers and lighting up remote communities, guided by its established vision, mission, and goals despite ongoing challenges.

BACKGROUND OF THE AGENCY
The Occidental Mindoro Electric Cooperative, Inc. (OMECO) was organized in 1974 through the initiative of Atty. Eufrocino L. Ramos, in line with Presidential Decree No. 269 which created the National Electrification Administration in 1969. OMECO began operations on May 7, 1974 after acquiring the San Jose Electric Company through a ₱6.9 million loan from NEA.

From nine initial employees, the cooperative steadily expanded under its first General Manager, Engr. Alberto A. Guiang, energizing more municipalities and acquiring other local electric systems. OMECO also led innovative efforts such as the Dendro Thermal Plant in Magsaysay, the first of its kind in Asia.

Despite a severe financial crisis in 1983, OMECO recovered under Col. Zoilo M. Perez (Ret.), who restored stability and earned recognition for efficient management. In 1997, members voted to remain under NEA to continue serving remote, non-profitable areas.
Now with over 25 years of service as of 2000, OMECO has grown into a vital institution in Occidental Mindoro, dedicated to delivering dependable and accessible electricity to member-consumers, especially in far-flung communities.
